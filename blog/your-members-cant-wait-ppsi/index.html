This week, America's Credit Unions sent a letter to the NCUA raising concerns that deserve attention from every credit union executive watching the GENIUS Act unfold. The core ask: give credit unions flexibility to invest in Permitted Payment Stablecoin Issuer (PPSI) subsidiaries that are structured differently from traditional CUSOs — and clarify whether PPSI reserve assets count against the existing 1% aggregate CUSO investment cap.

These are legitimate concerns. They are also concerns your credit union doesn't have to wait to resolve before serving members who hold crypto today.

The key distinction

Stablecoin issuance and crypto-collateralized lending are not the same thing. One requires a new federal license and months of regulatory clarity. The other is a loan — already within your credit union's existing lending authority, booked like any other collateralized product.

What the PPSI Debate Is Actually About

The GENIUS Act, signed into law in July 2025 and now entering its implementation phase, requires that federally insured credit unions wishing to issue payment stablecoins do so through a separately licensed subsidiary — a PPSI. For federal credit unions, the NCUA has interpreted this to mean a CUSO structure under Part 712.

That creates real structural friction. CUSOs are built around service organizations that primarily serve credit union members. Stablecoin issuers are reserve-backed payment infrastructure. ACU's letter correctly identifies that the CUSO rulebook wasn't written for this use case — and asks the NCUA to consider tailored treatment.

Then there is the 1% question. Federal credit unions are capped at investing 1% of paid-in and unimpaired capital and surplus in CUSOs. If PPSI reserve assets — the collateral backing the stablecoins themselves — count against that cap, the participation economics for any credit union outside the top tier become deeply unfavorable.

The NCUA has acknowledged this tension and is soliciting comment. Final regulations are due by July 18, 2026. Until then, credit unions considering the stablecoin path are in a genuine regulatory holding pattern.

Meanwhile, Your Members Are Already Here

While the regulatory machinery turns, the members in your branches — and in your loan queues — aren't waiting. They hold Bitcoin. They hold Ethereum. And right now, when they need liquidity, they have two choices: sell their crypto and trigger a taxable event, or go to a non-bank lender who charges them 15–20% to borrow against it.

Neither outcome serves your member. Neither outcome serves your credit union.
