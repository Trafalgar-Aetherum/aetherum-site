When a member pledges digital assets against a loan, that collateral has to sit somewhere. The question almost nobody asks is whether it has to sit still.

For most of the last decade the answer was effectively yes — not because the technology required it, but because the regulatory picture made anything else unexaminable. That has changed. And the change matters more for credit unions than for almost anyone else in financial services, because credit unions are the institutions least able to take on unquantified risk and most in need of new, member-aligned revenue.

The Tension Nobody Should Paper Over

Staking and collateralized lending pull against each other. That is the honest starting point.

The safety promise a lender makes to itself, to its examiner, and to its members is that pledged collateral can be liquidated quickly when a loan breaches its loan-to-value threshold. Staked assets, by default, cut against that promise in two ways. They are subject to unbonding and exit queues, so they are not instantly sellable. And they are subject to slashing, so the quantity of collateral is not perfectly fixed.

A platform that leads with the yield number and buries the mechanics is not a platform a credit union should adopt.

How the Risk Actually Gets Resolved

There are two defensible structures, and Aetherum supports both as policy choices the credit union makes — not defaults we impose.

Two Policy Structures, Both Examinable
Unstaked by default
Pledged collateral is held in its base form, fully liquid, with staking available only as an explicit member opt-in on a per-loan basis. Simplest to examine, simplest to explain, and the right starting posture for a first program.
Liquid staking form
Collateral is held in a liquid staking representation, which remains transferable and redeemable throughout the life of the loan. The asset continues to accrue protocol rewards while it serves as collateral, and the lender retains a liquidation path that does not depend on an exit queue clearing.

In both structures, the loan-to-value engine applies an explicit haircut for exit-queue latency and slashing exposure. That haircut is a parameter, it is versioned, and it is visible. Liquidation is gated through a pre-signing transaction policy layer, so no liquidation executes outside the credit union's own written policy — and every attempt, permitted or blocked, is recorded.

This is the part that is genuinely different. The staking mechanics are not novel; they are public infrastructure. What is novel is that every staking action, every reward accrual, every LTV recalculation, and every liquidation decision is emitted as a structured, examiner-readable event at the moment it happens. Not reconstructed at quarter-end from logs. Recorded as it occurs, because the system was built for the examiner as a first-class user.

The Regulatory Ground Has Moved

Three developments over the past eighteen months changed what is possible here.

In May 2025, the SEC's Division of Corporation Finance issued a statement concluding that solo, delegated, and custodial protocol staking activities do not constitute the offer or sale of securities where the provider's role is administrative or ministerial. In August 2025, a follow-on statement extended that reasoning to certain liquid staking activities and staking receipt tokens — the precise structure that makes productive collateral viable. Both are staff statements rather than binding rules, and both draw a hard boundary: restaking, provider-managed returns, and guaranteed yields fall outside their scope and remain legally uncertain. Aetherum operates inside that boundary deliberately and structurally, not as a matter of policy that could quietly drift.

On the credit union side, the NCUA has confirmed that federally insured credit unions may partner with third-party providers of digital asset services, and that such partnerships are evaluated under the same third-party risk framework as any other vendor relationship. The agency's subsequent guidance eliminating reputation risk as a standalone supervisory consideration removed the older examiner reflex of penalizing digital asset exposure on optics alone. Meanwhile the GENIUS Act and the rulemaking that followed have pulled digital asset supervision into a defined, familiar shape.

The practical translation: a credit union evaluating this today is running a vendor diligence process, not seeking permission.

What It Means for the Member

A member who has built a position in digital assets and needs liquidity has historically had one option — sell. Selling means a taxable disposal and permanent loss of the upside they were holding for in the first place.

Borrowing against the position removes both problems. The member keeps the asset and keeps the exposure. And where the credit union has enabled it and the member has opted in, the pledged asset continues to earn protocol rewards rather than sitting inert for the life of the loan.

A necessary caution, stated plainly: protocol rewards are variable, they are not guaranteed by Aetherum or by the credit union, and collateral remains subject to liquidation if the loan breaches its threshold. Any member-facing program built on this needs to say so in exactly those terms. A product that requires soft language to be attractive is not a product a credit union should put in front of its members.

The Distinction Worth Drawing

There is a category of company reporting large staking revenue on its own balance sheet right now, and the headlines are easy to confuse with what is described here. They are not the same activity. Those are treasury companies staking assets they own, for their own account.

Aetherum does not own the collateral, does not custody it, and does not earn a return on member assets. The credit union holds the relationship. The member holds the asset. Aetherum is the software and compliance layer that makes the arrangement legible to an examiner.

That distinction is not a limitation — it is the entire reason a regulated institution can participate at all.

Where This Goes

Productive collateral is the natural next step for digital asset lending, and the regulatory clarity to build it responsibly now exists. The constraint is no longer whether it is permitted. It is whether the infrastructure can prove, continuously and to a skeptical examiner, that the risk is bounded and the controls are real.
