The missing layer in regulated onchain credit — and what we built to close it.

Scroll

Onchain credit has a maturity problem. The instruments exist — tokenized notes, programmable coupons, transparent settlement. The rails exist — chains that clear in seconds, not days. The demand exists on both sides of the table: borrowers who want cheaper capital, and capital that wants better instruments to hold it in. What doesn’t exist yet, for the institutions that need it most, is the audit layer. Not more code. Not more speed. An audit-native foundation that a regulated institution can actually stand on.

THE LAYER THAT WAS MISSING

For a corporate issuer, onchain debt has already become unremarkable — in the best way. A note gets tokenized, a coupon schedule gets encoded, capital moves without a syndicate desk or a week of settlement float. No intermediary is needed to confirm a transfer happened, because the ledger already confirms it. That version of onchain credit is boring now. Boring is the compliment.

For a federally regulated depository institution, none of that exists. Not because the technology doesn’t work for them too — it does. It’s absent because nobody built the other half: the compliance story that survives an exam, and the audit trail that lets an examiner verify a transfer was permissible before it settled, rather than reconstruct it after the fact from a block explorer. A credit union can’t hold or issue a debt instrument on rails that only explain themselves to a developer. It needs rails that explain themselves to a regulator. That’s the layer that was missing — and closing it is a different engineering problem than making settlement fast.

THREE THINGS THAT HAVE TO BE TRUE

For a debt instrument to be usable by a regulated depository institution — not a lookalike, not a pilot running on training wheels — three things have to be true at the same time.

A
Transfer-Layer Eligibility Gating Every transfer checks the regulatory “eligible organization” test onchain, as a condition of settlement — not a generic KYC flag checked once at onboarding.
B
Policy-Gated Default A missed coupon or an LTV breach doesn’t trigger automatic liquidation. It routes through a pre-signing policy engine first — and a denial leaves the instrument active, with the reason on record.
C
Examiner-Native Audit Emission Every lifecycle event becomes a compliance receipt an examiner can read directly — not a log a vendor has to translate for them.

Transfer-layer gating is the first requirement, and it’s more specific than it sounds. Generic KYC tells you who someone is. It doesn’t tell you whether that entity meets the regulatory definition of an eligible organization for a given transaction — the actual test an examiner is going to ask about. We built that test into the transfer layer itself: eligibility isn’t a form filed once at onboarding, it’s a condition checked at the moment of settlement, every time.

Policy-gated default is the second, and it’s the one that breaks from how most crypto lending works. In a permissionless market, a missed coupon or an LTV breach past threshold is a trigger — collateral moves, automatically, in a single transaction. That’s fine for a permissionless market. It is not fine for a regulated depository institution, where liquidation is a decision with its own compliance obligations, not just a smart-contract condition. So a breach doesn’t fire a liquidation. It fires a policy check. A pre-signing policy engine evaluates the event against the institution’s own rules before any transaction is permitted to execute — and if the answer is no, the instrument stays active and the system records exactly why.

“A missed coupon or an LTV breach isn’t a liquidation trigger. It’s a question for the policy engine — and the answer, either way, gets written down.”

Examiner-native audit emission is the third, and it’s the one that makes the first two matter. An eligibility check or a policy denial is only as good as an examiner’s ability to see it. Every lifecycle event on the instrument — issuance, coupon payment, eligibility check, policy denial, liquidation — writes a compliance receipt built to be read directly, not translated by a vendor first.

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR WHERE THE MARKET IS GOING

Regulated depository institutions are balance-sheet constrained in a way most onchain credit participants aren’t. A credit union can’t underwrite more loans just because demand exists — capital, reserves, and concentration limits all cap what sits on the books. That constraint is exactly why outside capital reaching regulated loan pools matters. Not by routing around the regulatory perimeter, but by settling inside it — legally, cleanly, with an audit trail an examiner can verify.

That market isn’t built yet. It’s coming into focus. When it exists at scale, the institutions that can show a transfer layer gated on eligibility, a default process gated on policy, and an audit trail an examiner can read without a translator will be the ones capital actually wants to reach. This is the infrastructure for the market coming next — built ahead of it, not in response to it.

Audit-native isn’t a marketing phrase. It’s the condition that has to be true before a regulated institution can move onchain at all — not faster settlement, not cheaper capital, but a record an examiner trusts without having to trust us first.
